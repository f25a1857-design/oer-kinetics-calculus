Mathematical Framework

Cumulative Yield Model f(t)

We apply a modified asymmetrical growth model matching the empirical Malaysian estate productivity profiles:

$$f(t) = \frac{L}{1 + e^{-k(t - t_0)}}$$
  • L = Maximum annual biological potential yield
  • k = Aggressive yield development coefficient
  • t₀ = Point of maximum productivity acceleration
First Derivative f'(t) - Instantaneous Growth Rate

Applying the Quotient and Chain Rules, the derivative tells us how quickly the crop's yield output changes in real-time each year:

$$f'(t) = \frac{L \cdot k \cdot e^{-k(t - t_0)}}{\left(1 + e^{-k(t - t_0)}\right)^2}$$

Biological Interpretation: Unlike algebraic averages, $f'(t)$ provides the precise productivity momentum at age $t$. In agro-industry operations, this allows block-managers to calculate fertilizer response efficiency and allocate labor dynamically.

🌴 The Bioscience & Agronomic Problem

In Malaysian oil palm agribusiness, underperforming age profiles represent significant losses. As oil palms grow, their physical structures go through a dramatic shift. Young palms (3–7 years) spend resources on horizontal root anchoring and expanding frond surfaces. Peak crop yields occur around age 9 to 18, after which structural aging (senescence) and towering height increase harvesting costs while depressing fruit bunch production rate.

"How do plantation operators decide when a field is no longer economically viable to maintain?"

The answer lies in finding the point where the rate of change $f'(t)$ drops beneath a threshold value relative to high harvesting labor and fertilizer costs. By using derivatives, agronomists can plan replanting regimes precisely—ensuring Malaysian agricultural land remains highly productive.
